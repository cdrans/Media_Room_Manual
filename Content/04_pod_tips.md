# Tips & Tricks

The podcast station offers something the two video stations cannot: a format built on conversation rather than presentation. Where the greenscreen rewards a tight script and the lightboard rewards careful spatial planning, the podcast rewards preparation of yet another kind — knowing your questions, your structure, and your guests well enough that you can afford to be spontaneous. The station is set up for one host and up to two guests, and everything below is written with that configuration in mind.

## A Podcast Is Not a Recorded Conversation

The most common mistake at this station is to hit record on an unprepared chat and trust that the natural chemistry of a good conversation will survive on tape. It rarely does. Your listeners were not in the room: the shared context, the eye contact, and the whiteboard you glanced at are all invisible to them. A discussion that felt lively in person often comes across as unfocused audio.

At the same time, do not over-correct into the opposite mistake. The reason to choose this station over the video stations is precisely the things a scripted video removes: dialogue, disagreement, thinking aloud, multiple voices, and nuance. A podcast lets your audience listen while commuting or exercising, and listeners will happily stay with you far longer than video viewers will — *if* the conversation stays purposeful.

Be honest with yourself about the format's limits before you book the station:

- There is **no visual channel at all**. No slides, no equations, no pointing at a diagram. If your content requires visual reasoning, it belongs at the lightboard or greenscreen station instead.
- Everything must be carried by **voice and language**. "As you can see in this graph" is a sentence you can never say here — you must describe, compare, and summarise in words.
- Audio is unforgiving about **sound discipline**. A rustling sleeve or a tapped table that a camera would never notice is glaring in a listener's headphones.

```{tip}
A quick decision guide: choose the **greenscreen** when you are presenting prepared material with visuals, the **lightboard** when you are deriving or building something up step by step, and the **podcast station** when the value lies in the exchange between people — interviews, discussions, reflections, and stories.
```

## What We Can Learn from Podcasters

The lessons from professional YouTubers in the [Greenscreen Station](learn_from_youtubers) chapter — the hook, pacing, and signposting — apply here too, but each of them translates differently to audio.

**The cold open still rules.** The first minute decides whether a listener stays. Open with the most interesting tension, question, or short clip from later in the episode — not with housekeeping, introductions of the department, or "welcome to episode twelve of...". Introductions can come *after* you have given the listener a reason to care.

**Signposting matters even more in audio.** A viewer can skim a video timeline; a listener cannot skim sound. Explicit verbal structure is their only navigation: "We'll cover three things today...", "That brings us to the second question...", "Before we wrap up, let's return to...". What would feel heavy-handed in writing is a courtesy in audio.

**Length works differently here.** This is the one place in this manual where "shorter is almost always better" gets some nuance. Podcast listeners tolerate — and often prefer — longer episodes than video viewers, because they listen while doing something else. But length is only earned by focus.

```{tip}
A useful rule of thumb: it is not the episode that must be short, but every **segment** that must earn its place. A focused 40-minute conversation beats a meandering 20-minute one. If a segment does not serve the episode's core question, cut it — in the plan if possible, in the edit if necessary.
```

## Preparing Without Over-Scripting

```{warning}
The full-script advice from the [Greenscreen Station](sec:scripting) does **not** transfer to this station. A read script sounds read. Reading dialogue aloud kills exactly the conversational quality that justified choosing this format in the first place.
```

What replaces the script is an **episode plan** — the podcast equivalent of the lightboard's [board plan](sec:board_plan). Before recording, write down:

- The **core question** of the episode, in one sentence. If you cannot, the episode is not ready.
- The **segments**, each with a target duration and the key question or point that anchors it.
- Your **planned transitions** between segments — these are the moments most likely to derail an unprepared conversation.
- The **intended takeaway**: what should a listener be able to say the episode was about?

For interview episodes, preparation extends to your guests:

- Hold a short **pre-interview** conversation (even fifteen minutes by phone) so you know where the interesting material is and your guests know what to expect.
- **Order your questions from concrete to abstract.** Guests warm up on specifics ("What happened in that first experiment?") and only then speak well about the big picture ("What does this mean for the field?").
- **Brief both guests on the format**: episode length, whether mistakes can be re-taken, and that pauses are fine — silence is edited out, but talking over each other is not.

The exception is the **solo, narrated episode** — a monologue or audio essay. These *are* scripted, and the scripting guidance from the greenscreen chapter applies almost unchanged: write for the ear in short sentences, plan for roughly 120–150 spoken words per minute, and mark emphasis and pauses in the script.

```{admonition} Try for yourself!
:class: tip
Pick a topic you know well. Record two minutes on it twice: once reading a fully written script, once speaking from five bullet points. Listen back to both. Almost everyone finds the bullet-point version more engaging — and almost everyone also finds it needs a clearer structure. The episode plan is how you get the best of both.
```

## Microphone and Voice Technique

The station's microphones are close-talking microphones: they sound excellent at a consistent, close distance and mediocre everywhere else.

- **Keep a constant distance and angle** to your microphone — roughly a hand's width away, speaking across rather than directly into it. Do not lean back when thinking or lean in when excited; your levels will swing wildly.
- **Stay on-mic when you turn.** The natural instinct is to turn your head toward the guest you are addressing. Turn your body and chair instead, so your mouth stays aimed at your own microphone.
- **Listen silently.** Nodding, smiling, and eye contact tell your guest you are engaged — the microphone hears none of it, and that is exactly the point. The "mm-hm" and "right, right" that feel polite in person land on your guest's words as crosstalk and are nearly impossible to edit out. Silent listening is this station's equivalent of the lightboard's "put the marker down."
- **Control the small noises.** No paper shuffling (use a tablet or single stiff sheet), no pens, no tapping the table the microphones stand on, phones in flight mode and off the table, and water in a glass rather than a crackling bottle.
- **Record with the edit in mind.** If you fumble a sentence, do not apologise and plough on — pause for two seconds, then restart the sentence cleanly from its beginning. The pause gives the editor a clean cut point. Before or after the session, record thirty seconds of everyone sitting silently ("room tone"); it is invaluable for smoothing edits later.

```{note}
With three people at the table, each on their own microphone, the biggest technical win is simply **one person speaking at a time**. Overlapping speech from separate microphones cannot be untangled in editing.
```

## Hosting the Conversation

With one host and two guests, the host's job is less "asking questions" and more "conducting."

- **Ask one question at a time.** Stacked questions ("How did the project start, and what surprised you, and would you do it again?") get one answer — usually to the easiest part.
- **Let silence work.** After a guest finishes, wait a beat before jumping in. Often the best material comes in the sentence they add to fill that silence. Resist the urge to rescue every pause.
- **Follow the conversation, not the list.** Your question list is a safety net, not a march route. The best follow-up question is usually inside the answer you just heard.
- **Direct traffic by name.** With two guests, address questions explicitly: "Anna, you saw this from the industry side — how did it look to you?" This prevents the awkward who-goes-first glance (which the listener experiences as dead air) and lets you deliberately bring in the quieter guest.
- **Balance the airtime.** It is the host's job, not the guests', to notice when one voice is dominating. A gentle "I want to bring [name] in on this" fixes it without friction.
- **Land the ending.** Close with a brief verbal summary of the takeaway and a clear outro. In audio, the summary is not repetition — it is the listener's only chance to "see" the structure of what they just heard. This is the podcast's version of the greenscreen chapter's closing signpost.

## Clothing and What the Microphone Hears

```{warning}
The clothing recommendations in this manual differ per station. The advice below applies to the podcast station only — and it works on a completely different principle than at the video stations.
```

Since this station records audio only, your clothing is chosen for **sound, not sight**. Nobody will ever see your outfit; everyone will hear it.

- **Avoid rustling fabrics** — nylon, stiff rain jackets, and windbreakers broadcast every movement. Soft cotton and wool are effectively silent.
- **Leave noisy jewelry off the table** — literally. Bangles, loose bracelets, and long necklaces click against the table and microphone stand. Take them off or push them out of reach before recording.
- **Mind the chair and shoes.** Squeaky chairs and shuffling feet are picked up more than you expect. Settle into a comfortable position before recording and plant your feet.
- **Lanyards and badges** tap against the table edge every time you lean in. Take them off.

There is a silver lining: green clothing, small patterns, and reflective fabrics — all forbidden at the other stations — are entirely welcome here.
