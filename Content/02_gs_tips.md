# Tips & Tricks
Before you step in front of the camera, it is worth pausing to think about *what kind of video you are making* and *how you want your audience to experience it*. The greenscreen station is a powerful tool, but the quality of the recording equipment is only one part of what makes a successful learning video. This chapter covers the habits, decisions, and techniques that separate forgettable recordings from ones that students actually finish watching.

## Educational Video ≠ Recorded Lecture
The most common mistake staff make when first using the greenscreen station is approaching it as though they are delivering a lecture. It is an understandable instinct, but it tends to produce videos that are too long, too loosely structured, and difficult for students to navigate. Additionally, *lecturing* to a camera is certainly not the same experiences as lecturing to an audience, so much of your charisma and stage presence you are accustomed to in a classroom setting does not naturally manifest the same way in a room by yourself in front of a camera.

A lecture works in a live context because of the social contract in the room: students are present, there is a shared rhythm, and the lecturer can read the audience and adjust. None of those dynamics exist in a video. A student watching alone has a pause button, a back button, and (unfortunately) often a different tab open competing for their attention.

The greenscreen format rewards a different approach entirely:

- **Shorter is almost always better.** A ten-minute video covering one concept clearly will be watched more often and more fully than a forty-minute recording covering five. If your content is substantial, consider breaking it into a short series of focused episodes rather than one long recording.
- **Structure is visible, not implied.** In a lecture, a skilled speaker guides an audience through transitions naturally. In a video, those transitions need to be made explicit — a brief statement of what the video will cover, clear signposting as you move between points, and a concise closing summary go a long way.
- **Every minute must earn its place.** In a room, a moment of thinking aloud or reviewing notes feels natural. On video, it reads as padding. A tightly scripted and rehearsed greenscreen video will almost always outperform a loosely improvised one of the same length.

Think of each greenscreen video not as a lecture captured on film, but as a self-contained learning object — something more like a section of a textbook than a class session.

## What We Can Learn from YouTubers

This may seem like an unusual place to look for pedagogical inspiration, but educational content creators on platforms like YouTube have spent years solving exactly the problem you are facing: how do you hold a viewer's attention voluntarily, without the social pressure of a classroom, on a topic they may not yet care about?

A few techniques that translate directly to academic video production:

### The Hook

The first thirty seconds of a video determine whether most viewers will stay or leave. Experienced video creators open with something that immediately signals *why this matters* — a question the viewer wants answered, a counterintuitive claim, a striking image, or a concrete problem that the video will resolve. This is called a **hook**. You can see an example of this in the first 40 seconds of the video below:

A hook does not have to be dramatic or performative. For academic content, it might be as simple as opening with a question students commonly find puzzling, a real-world scenario the concept explains, or a statement that productively challenges an assumption they are likely to hold. It should create some tension or conflict that will be resolved over the course of the video. What it should never be is a slow preamble: resist the urge to introduce yourself at length, explain the course structure, or apologise for anything before you have given the viewer a reason to keep watching.

:::{note}
**Exploring hooks further**

The [Science Hooks resource series](https://sciencehooks.scoilnet.ie/) — developed by science educators and co-funded by the National University of Ireland, Galway — offers an excellent collection of short video hooks across chemistry, physics, and biology topics. Even if your discipline is different, browsing a few examples is a practical way to develop an instinct for what a well-constructed hook looks and feels like. The resource is freely available and carries a Creative Commons licence.
:::

### Pacing and Energy

Effective video presenters tend to speak slightly faster, and with slightly more deliberate emphasis, than they would in a room. This is because video flattens vocal dynamics — what feels expressive in person can read as flat on a recording. You do not need to adopt a performance persona, but being conscious of pace and vocal energy during recording makes a real difference to how engaging the final product feels.

### Signposting

YouTubers are unusually explicit about structure: "In this video, we're going to cover X, Y, and Z" at the start, and "So, to summarise what we've looked at…" at the end, are conventions that feel slightly formulaic but genuinely help viewers orient themselves. For academic content — where the material may be genuinely demanding — this kind of scaffolding is even more valuable than it is in casual educational content.

## Scripting Basics

The greenscreen format works best when you know exactly what you are going to say before you start recording. A full script — not just bullet points — is strongly recommended, particularly for your first few recordings. In this section, we will explore the reasoning for this and provide you some useful tips on how to go about scripting a video.

### Why a Full Script?
Experience has taught most lecturers that improvisation is actually a superpower for teaching in the classroom, and they would never dream to script every word they were going to say for a lecture. So why would you throw this experience out the window when making an educational video? There are two main reasons for this. First, we previously discussed the importance of the pacing and energy of the speaker in an educational video. The reality is that a written script gives you control over timing, language precision, and structure in a way that improvised delivery cannot. The second reason is best illustrated with the below exercise:

:::{tip} Try for yourself!
**Try the following:** Think about what you had for breakfast this morning. Once your memory is clear about your morning routine, pretend you are going to record a video describing your morning routine. Stare directly at the image of the camera below and describe your morning routine in detail, being sure to keep up the pacing and energy needed for an engaging video.

```{image} ./figures/pexels-dlxmedia-hu-215591835-11894291.jpg
:alt: fimage of a video camera from the perspective of a person being recorded.
:width: 80%
:align: center
```
:::

How did this exercise go? What did you find odd about it? What most people dont realize is the importance of having a real audience in front of you for being spontaneous. You are constantly observing the reaction of the audience to what you are saying and using those cues to make constant adjustments. In the absence of an audience, you have the blank stare of a camera that provide you no feedback. So the only audience is your own, often too critical in the moment, mind. Scripting allows you to remove this from the recording process and allows you to fully concentrate on the pacing and energy of your delivery!

### Practical Scripting Tips

- **Write for the ear, not the eye.** Read your script aloud as you draft it. Sentences that look fine on the page sometimes feel unnatural when spoken. Shorter sentences and contractions ("you'll" rather than "you will") tend to sound more natural on camera.
- **One idea per sentence.** Avoid long, nested constructions. If a sentence contains multiple clauses separated by commas, consider breaking it into two sentences.
- **Aim for approximately 120–150 words per minute** when estimating timing. A five-minute video is roughly 600–750 words of spoken content — much shorter than most people expect.
- **Mark your emphasis.** Underline or bold the words you want to stress in each sentence. This helps during delivery and ensures key terms land with the weight they deserve.
- **Build in visual cues.** Note in your script where a new slide should appear, where you want to gesture toward a diagram, or where a pause would be appropriate. Your script and your slide deck should be designed together, not separately.

:::{tip}Use voice-to-text apps to create your first draft
Most teachers in a technical field are experts at writing technical reports and documentation, while writing a script is more akin to writing a theatrical play. To avoid having the first draft of your script sound like a technical manual, why not use a voice-to-text app and produce your first draft by "lecturing" it out loud. Doing so allows you to capture elements of your spontaneous teaching persona, but also allows you to clean it up and optimize it for your final script.
:::

### Using a Teleprompter

The greenscreen station is equipped with a teleprompter, which displays your script on-screen while the camera records. It is highly recommended that you use the teleprompter, rather than memorizing the script, as it allows you to focus on pacing and has delivery of your text. It also has the added benefit of ensuring you maintain eye contact with the camera lens, which significantly improves the sense of presence and directness in the final recording.

## Clothing and Appearance for Greenscreen Recording

Greenscreen technology works by digitally removing a specific colour from the video image and replacing it with your chosen background (your slides, in this case). This process is very effective, but it does have one important implication for what you wear.

:::{warning}
The recommendations for clothing and appearance for the Greenscreen Station are not the same as for the Lightboard Station. Please see the chapter on the Lightboard Station for recommendations when using that station. 
:::

### What to Avoid

- **Green and lime tones.** Any clothing that is close in colour to the greenscreen will be partially or fully removed from the image. This includes not just bright green but also some yellows, khakis, and olive shades depending on the specific screen calibration.
- **Fine patterns.** Narrow stripes, small checks, herringbone, and similar fine-weave patterns can cause a visual flickering effect known as moiré on camera. These should be avoided regardless of colour.
- **Highly reflective fabrics.** Shiny or metallic materials can pick up unwanted reflections from the studio lighting and create inconsistent colour across the frame.

:::{figure} ./figures/gs_clothing_nos.png
:name: fig-clothingnos
:alt: Three panels each marked with a red cross illustrating clothing to avoid on greenscreen: a bright green t-shirt that blends into the backdrop; a striped shirt showing moiré wave distortion; and a shiny metallic jacket with glaring light reflections.
:width: 100%
:::


### What Works Well

- **Solid, mid-tone colours.** Blues, purples, reds, warm neutrals, and greys tend to work reliably in front of a greenscreen. These colours contrast clearly with the screen and render consistently under studio lighting.
- **Comfortable, well-fitted clothing.** You will likely be recording for longer than you expect. Clothing you are comfortable in allows you to focus on delivery rather than adjusting what you are wearing.
- **Consistency across a series.** If you are recording multiple videos that form a series, wearing the same or similar clothing across sessions gives the finished materials a coherent, professional appearance.
